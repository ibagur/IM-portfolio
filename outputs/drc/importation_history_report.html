The longitudinal panel spans 13 snapshots, one per change in the cumulative confirmed-case total, which grew 13 → 208 cases. Over that window 21 health zones became hotspots (ever-affected / Cat A).

The validation question — did zones flagged high on the importation watchlist later become hotspots? — is answered yes for the majority: 12 of the 21 hotspots carried a “High” importation ranking before their first confirmed case.

ℹ How to read “importance”
The importation index runs 0–100, where 100 = top of the watchlist; rank is the zone’s position among all non-hotspot zones at that snapshot.

Reading it

✓ Strongest precursor signals
Bunia and Karisimbi both sat at the very top of the watchlist (index 100, rank #1) and confirmed cases within 3 and 7 days respectively.

Several zones (Beni, Komanda, Logo) started modest but climbed the ranking over successive snapshots before turning hotspot — the gap between the first-flag index and the pre-hotspot peak shows this (e.g. Beni 8 → 83).

Lead times of 14–17 days are anchored to the panel’s start (2026-05-15), so for those zones the true lead is at least that long.

Caveats

⚠ Interpret with care
  • Of the 21 hotspots, 9 were not pre-flagged High: Rwampara was already a hotspot in the first snapshot (no observable lead), and the rest appeared with lower-tier or no prior watchlist ranking.
  • Importation is a relative network-proximity ranking, not a probability. This is a retrospective concordance check, not a forecast.
  • Hotspot = ever-affected (cumulative confirmed cases > 0), a sticky flag; it differs from the model’s windowed active-seed status.
