Purpose and scope

Read the outputs differently. The current dashboard model is a relative importation-risk ranking for surveillance prioritisation; its score is not a probability or a forecast of infection. The two Bayesian arms forecast a zone’s first reported confirmed case, but their 14-day posterior values are still experimental and must not be presented as calibrated operational probabilities.

The table below freezes all three lists at the last Sunday for which the Bayesian challenger was issued: 19 July 2026. The deterministic list uses its 14-day dashboard snapshot at that same cutoff. The Bayesian lists cover the following 14 days, 20 July–2 August.

The three methods

Operational baseline

Current deterministic risk model

  • Target: prioritise currently unaffected receiving zones by incoming risk from confirmed-case zones (Cat A sources).
  • Source intensity: square-root of recent incident cases, with a 5-day exponential recency decay; the shown list uses the 14-day window.
  • Mobility: row-normalised Flowminder/Vodacom outflow; uncovered origins use gravity proportional to destination population divided by travel time squared.
  • Output: arbitrary-scale score, rank and watchlist tier. It is a one-hop receiver ranking, not a probability.
risk(j) = Σᵢ seed_weight(i) × mobility(i → j)
Bayesian challenger · arm 1

Bayesian dashboard-pressure arm

  • Target: first reported confirmed case in a zone with no case at the Sunday cutoff; 7-day hazard and cumulative 14-day output.
  • Pressure input: the existing 14-day dashboard importation score, read from its history file without recalculating or changing the dashboard.
  • Additional structure: population, deprivation, health-site density, nearest affected-zone travel time, route-missing flag, province effect and a week-to-week baseline random walk.
  • Output: posterior first-confirmation watchlist and 5th–95th percentile interval.
cloglog(qᵢₜ) = baselineₜ + Xᵢₜβ + provinceᵢ
Bayesian challenger · arm 2

Bayesian article-pressure arm

  • Target and hazard model: identical to the dashboard-pressure arm, so the arms are comparable.
  • Pressure input: daily reported incidence is convolved with a generation-time distribution (mean 15.3 days; SD 9.3 days).
  • Mobility: a 24 May short-trip profile for Bunia, Mongbwalu and Rwampara; unobserved destinations and other origins receive gravity residuals/fallbacks.
  • Output: a separate posterior first-confirmation watchlist, based on this alternative pressure hypothesis but the same covariates and risk set.
pressure(j) = Σᵢ infectivity(i) × W(i → j)

What the Bayesian arms have in common

Comparable design

  • Same Sunday cutoffs, public INSP SitRep report-date target and never-confirmed-zone risk set.
  • A zone leaves the Bayesian risk set permanently after its first reported confirmation.
  • Same covariates, priors, discrete-time complementary-log-log hazard, four-chain fit and convergence gate.
  • Both 19 July fits passed the gate: R-hat ≤ 1.0021, adequate effective sample sizes, and zero divergences.

Important limitation

The prospective 14-day outcomes are not mature yet. The values in the next table are useful as experimental rankings and uncertainty summaries, not as calibrated probabilities of infection or a replacement for the dashboard’s operational watchlist.

Conclusion and recommendations

Brief conclusion

The dashboard-pressure challenger remains closely tied to the operational deterministic signal: its top-ranked zones are predominantly already high in the dashboard list, while the Bayesian model adds a first-confirmation target, covariates and posterior uncertainty. The article-pressure challenger tests a meaningfully different exposure assumption and brings Jiba, Linga, Karisimbi, Kirotshe and Yakusu higher in the watchlist.

There is not yet a mature prospective outcome window to declare either challenger better. The models should therefore be interpreted as complementary research watchlists, not competing operational truth claims.

Recommendations

  1. Keep the deterministic dashboard model as the operational baseline and do not alter its current pipeline.
  2. Run both Bayesian arms in shadow mode at each Sunday cutoff and retain their immutable diagnostic archives.
  3. Use consensus zones across lists as a focused analyst-review set; among the current top tens these are Adi, Biringi, Laybo, Nyarambe, Rethy and Watsa.
  4. Score the 19 July issue after 26 July (7-day) and 2 August (14-day), using recall, precision, Brier/log score and calibration checks.
  5. Do not promote posterior values to operational probabilities until several prospective cutoffs and sufficient first-invasion events demonstrate calibration and improvement over the baseline.
