Tawila IDP Camp — WASH Household Clustering Analysis

Unsupervised classification of 369 households across Water, Sanitation & Hygiene indicators • Sudan, February 2026

Key Finding: Two Distinct Population Groups Identified

The analysis identified two statistically distinct clusters of households with dramatically different WASH conditions. Cluster 1 ("High Vulnerability", 47%) faces severe, compounding deprivation across all WASH domains — low water availability, damaged/shared latrines, minimal soap, and high protection risks. Cluster 0 ("Moderate Needs", 53%) has significantly better access but still falls short of humanitarian standards. The differences are statistically significant across 17 of 22 key indicators (p<0.01).

Geographic Concentration: Deprivation Maps to Specific Camps

The two clusters are not randomly distributed — they are strongly concentrated by camp location (Chi-square p = 2.3×10-24). Camps B (SCI, 86% high-vulnerability) and C (TGH, 84%) are the epicentres of WASH deprivation, while Camp D (SI, 77% moderate-needs) is significantly better served. Camp A (IRC) is mixed (37% high-vulnerability, 63% moderate). This means response prioritisation can be geographically targeted.

🔬 Alternative View: K=3 Clustering — Differentiating Types of Deprivation

What K=3 Reveals: The High-Vulnerability Group Has Two Distinct Sub-Profiles

Splitting into three clusters preserves the "Low Need" group (43%) but separates the deprived households into two sub-groups with very different response needs: Cluster 2 "Multi-Sector Crisis" (39%) — dominated by water scarcity: 80% report water problems, 80% spend >31 min fetching, median 10.7 L/p/d. Largest HH (8.5 people), 50% have disabilities. Concentrated in Camp C (93%). Cluster 1 "Sanitation Crisis" (18%) — dominated by sanitation infrastructure failure: 94% report sanitation problems, 88% observe open defecation, 71% have damaged latrines, 68% feel unsafe. Concentrated in Camp B (54%). Their top concern is sanitation (39%), not water. This distinction is actionable: Camp C needs water infrastructure; Camp B needs latrine rehabilitation.

🎯 Recommendations for Humanitarian Response

1. Priority Water Interventions — Focus on Camps B & C

Cluster 0 households receive only 10 L/p/d median (vs. Sphere minimum of 15 L/p/d). Camp C is most critical at 6.6 L/p/d, Camp B at 10.3 L/p/d. 79% of Cluster 0 reports water access problems, and 57% spend over 31 minutes fetching water. Action: Prioritize water trucking and additional water points for Camps B (SCI) and C (TGH). Camp C urgently needs container distribution given its extremely low per-capita consumption. Consider 13% of Cluster 0 relying on unprotected wells for targeted water treatment.

2. Sanitation Infrastructure — Critical in Camps B & C

Camps B and C have 84-86% shared latrines, 67% report damaged facilities (Camp B), and 70% feel unsafe (Camp C). Open defecation observation is 75% in Camp B. Action: Emergency latrine construction/rehabilitation and desludging in Camps B and C. Installation of lighting and locks is critical for protection, particularly in Camp C where 70% feel unsafe. Gender-segregated facilities are a priority. Camp A also needs attention (61% sanitation problems).

3. Hygiene Kit Distribution — Camp C Most Acute

Only 1.8% of Camp C has enough soap — virtually none. Camp B is at 8.7%. Camp B has 96% reporting hygiene item problems. Handwashing with water and soap is available to only 17% of Cluster 0. Action: Immediate targeted hygiene kit distribution to Camps C (TGH) and B (SCI), with soap, menstrual hygiene materials, and water treatment products. Prioritize households with children under 5 (75% of Cluster 0) and PLW (65%).

4. Geographic Targeting Strategy

The strong geographic concentration of vulnerability (Camps B: 86% and C: 84% high-vulnerability vs. Camp D: 23%) enables efficient area-based targeting. Action: Camps B and C should be treated as priority areas for all WASH interventions. Camp D (SI) can serve as a model for what improved conditions look like. Camp A (IRC) needs mixed-approach programming given its 37% high-vulnerability households are interspersed with better-off ones. Coordinate with SCI (Camp B), TGH (Camp C), and IRC (Camp A) on differentiated response packages.

5. Vulnerability-Based Targeting Within Camps

Cluster 0 has significantly higher vulnerability: larger household sizes (8.4 vs 6.6), more children under 5 (75% vs 63%), more PLW (65% vs 45%), higher malnutrition treatment (26% vs 11%), and more disabilities (46% vs 24%). Action: Within the priority camps, use these demographic markers for further beneficiary prioritization. The compounding nature of vulnerabilities in Cluster 0 suggests these households need integrated, multi-sector support beyond WASH alone.

Methodology

Data: 369 households surveyed across 4 camp sections in Tawila IDP camp, Sudan (February 2026). Survey covers Water Supply, Sanitation, Solid Waste Management, and Hygiene domains.

Feature Selection: 70 features selected from 234 survey variables, covering demographics (14), water supply (24), sanitation (11), solid waste (4), and hygiene (17). Categorical variables encoded as ordinal or binary. Missing values imputed with median (continuous) or 0 (binary flags for conditional questions).

Clustering: K-Means clustering with PCA dimensionality reduction (80% variance retained). Optimal K=2 selected via silhouette analysis (score=0.595). Validated against hierarchical clustering (Ward linkage) with 86% agreement. All 17 key indicator differences are statistically significant (Kruskal-Wallis, p<0.01).

Geographic Analysis: Post-hoc Chi-square test confirms clusters are strongly associated with camp location (Chi-square=113.2, p=2.3×10-24, df=3). Camps B (SCI) and C (TGH) are predominantly Cluster 0; Camp D (SI) is predominantly Cluster 1; Camp A (IRC) is mixed.

Limitations: Clustering captures the dominant axis of variation (WASH deprivation severity). Further sub-clustering within each group may reveal additional nuance. Self-reported data may have response biases. The 15 L/p/d Sphere standard is referenced as a minimum benchmark. The geographic concentration could partly reflect differing survey methodologies between organisations, though the consistency across multiple independent indicators suggests real differences.
